A smoke detector is a device that senses smoke, typically as an indicator of fire. Commercial smoke detectors issue a signal to a fire alarm control panel as part of a fire alarm system, while household smoke detectors, also known as smoke alarms, generally issue an audible or visual alarm from the detector itself or several detectors if there are multiple smoke detectors interlinked.

Smoke detectors are housed in plastic enclosures, typically shaped like a disk about 150 millimetres (6 in) in diameter and 25 millimetres (1 in) thick, but shape and size vary. Smoke can be detected either optically (photoelectric) or by physical process (ionization). Detectors may use one of the two, or both sensing methods. Sensitive alarms can be used to detect, and deter, people smoking in areas where smoking is banned.

Are smart smoke detectors worth it?

Smart Smoke Alarms Don't Necessarily Offer Better Smoke Detection. Even the Nest Protect's “split-spectrum” photoelectric sensor isn't vastly better than a regular smoke alarm that comes with both ionization and photoelectric smoke sensors.

Where do you place a smart smoke detector?

The device installs on the ceiling or high on the wall (typically replacing an old-school smoke alarm in the same location), either using batteries or in-wall power.
